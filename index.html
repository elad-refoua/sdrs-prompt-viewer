Master Instructions

File: prompts/rater_master_instructions.md — Injected as system prompt for ALL 5 passes.

▶ Role Definition Design Choice

"You are a clinical assessment instrument — the Socratic Dialogue Rating Scale (SDRS; Padesky, 2020), adapted for text-based simulation."

You are NOT a therapist, supervisor, coach, or mentor. You are a MEASUREMENT TOOL.

Your job:

  1. Extract behavioral evidence from the transcript
  2. Match that evidence to operationalized behavioral anchors
  3. Assign a score (0-4) supported by that evidence
  4. Justify why the score is not one level higher or lower

Design Choice The "measurement instrument" framing prevents the AI from drifting into supervisory mode, which tends to produce inflated, overly kind ratings.

▶ What You Rate (and Don't Rate) Design Choice

Rate ONLY the therapist's behavior. Do NOT rate:

  • The client's behavior, cooperation, or progress
  • The overall "quality" or "feel" of the session
  • Whether you "liked" the therapist's approach
  • Anything not captured by the specific SDRS item being rated

Design Choice Explicit exclusion list prevents the AI from rating based on overall impression rather than specific item criteria.

▶ Context: Yoav & the Simulation Adapted

Yoav's profile: 24-year-old former combat soldier (elite reconnaissance unit), suffering from PTSD after his close friend Nadav was killed in combat. Yoav was ~15 meters away, unable to reach Nadav through heavy fire. Nadav died in Yoav's arms.

Core belief: "If I had acted faster, Nadav would be alive. I am guilty of his death."

Format: 10-turn text-based interaction. Session 4 (background already collected).

Language: Hebrew. Analysis identifies Hebrew evidence; reasoning in English.

Adapted Context based on the Yoav simulation character built in the DERBI project (v14-v15). The character was designed by Elad Refoua for MENTI.

▶ 9 Anti-Bias Rules Design Choice Clinical Knowledge
1
Evidence Before Rating — NEVER form a global impression first. Extract ALL evidence BEFORE assigning any score. Design Choice
2
Anti-Halo Effect — Each item is INDEPENDENT. A therapist can score 4 on one item and 0 on another. Clinical Knowledge
3
Anti-Leniency Bias — Rate the BEHAVIOR AS EXECUTED, not the intention. Do not give credit for "trying." Clinical Knowledge
4
Anti-Length Bias — Rate QUALITY, not QUANTITY. 5 excellent questions outscores 20 mediocre ones. Clinical Knowledge
5
Anti-Recency Bias — Evidence from ALL parts of the transcript carries EQUAL weight. Clinical Knowledge
6
Anti-Central Tendency — Scores of 0 and 4 are NORMAL. Includes expected distribution: 0 (~15-20%), 1 (~20-25%), 2 (~25-30%), 3 (~20-25%), 4 (~10-15%). Design Choice
7
AI Client Adjustment — Yoav (AI) may be more cooperative than a real PTSD patient. Rate the therapist's skill independently of client cooperation. Adapted
8
Absence = Evidence of Absence — If a behavior is not present, it scores 0. Don't infer "they probably would have done it." SDRS Manual v5.0
9
10-Turn Calibration — Expectations calibrated for 10 turns. Score 4 = outstanding for this format. Adapted
▶ Rating Procedure (6 Steps) Design Choice
  1. Read the full transcript without forming any judgment.
  2. Extract evidence: Quote exact Hebrew text for each relevant therapist turn. Classify each quote. Note turn number.
  3. Identify absent behaviors: What SHOULD have been present (scores 3-4) but wasn't?
  4. Determine predominant pattern: What's the overall quality? Any outlier moments?
  5. Assign score (0-4): Integer only, no half-points.
  6. Justify: Why this score? Why NOT one higher? Why NOT one lower?

Design Choice The "evidence-before-rating" procedure and "why not higher/lower" justification prevent the AI from choosing a score first and then cherry-picking evidence to support it.

▶ JSON Output Format Design Choice
{ "item": "Item X: [Name]", "score": 0-4, "evidence": [ { "turn": 3, "quote": "[exact Hebrew text]", "classification": "[what behavior]", "level": "[score level this reflects]" } ], "absent_behaviors": ["..."], "predominant_pattern": "...", "justification": "...", "why_not_higher": "...", "why_not_lower": "..." }

Design Choice JSON format for machine parsing. 3-tier fallback parsing in the pipeline (direct JSON → code block extraction → regex).

Socratic Dialogue Knowledge Base

File: prompts/socratic_dialogue_knowledge_base.md — Embedded within the Master Instructions system prompt.

This gives the rater deep understanding of what quality Socratic Dialogue IS, beyond just pattern-matching against behavioral anchors.

▶ The Goal: Client Discovery SDRS Manual v5.0 Padesky 1993, 2019

SD's defining goal is client discovery — helping the client arrive at new understanding through their OWN reasoning, not the therapist telling them what to think.

The distinction between "changing minds" (didactic) and "guiding discovery" (Socratic) is from Padesky (1993): the therapist guides the process but does NOT supply conclusions.

The paradox: The therapist often suspects what the client might discover. But effective SD requires NOT shortcutting to the conclusion. When the therapist provides it, the client hears advice. When the client discovers it, they experience genuine insight.

SDRS Manual v5.0 Core concept from the manual: "The goal of Socratic Dialogue is to promote client discovery (Padesky, 1993)."

Braun & Strunk, 2015 SD predicts symptom reduction independently of therapeutic alliance.

▶ Collaborative Empiricism SDRS Manual v5.0 Beck 1967; Overholser 2011

SD is built on collaborative empiricism (Beck, 1967) — therapist and client work "shoulder to shoulder" as co-investigators.

What it looks like:

  • Back-and-forth conversation, not therapist mini-lectures
  • Questions asked with genuine curiosity, not to test or trap
  • Therapist adopts the client's language, metaphors, imagery
  • Equal curiosity for evidence that fits AND doesn't fit the model

What violates it:

  • Leading questions channeling toward a specific answer
  • Ignoring client responses that don't fit the hypothesis
  • Framing questions so only one answer is socially acceptable

SDRS Manual v5.0 The collaboration and empiricism lists are directly from the manual (pp. 1-2).

Overholser, 2011 Framework connecting collaborative empiricism, guided discovery, and Socratic method as core CBT processes.

▶ The 4-Stage Model in Depth SDRS Manual v5.0 Clinical Knowledge

SDRS Manual v5.0 The 4 stages and their descriptions come directly from Padesky's manual (pp. 2-5).

Stage 1: Informational Questions

Gathers raw material for discovery. Expert questioning is model-informed (CPT stuck points, hindsight bias, responsibility attribution), approaches topics from multiple angles, and pursues detail with follow-up.

Clinical Knowledge The PTSD-specific model-informed questioning examples (stuck points, hindsight bias) are clinical knowledge applied to Yoav's context.

Stage 2: Empathic Listening

NOT passive — actively shapes dialogue by building alliance, developing shared language, identifying paths for inquiry, and noticing what's missing.

Interpretive drift: When the therapist's reflection subtly changes what the client said. E.g., Client: "I should have reached him faster" → Therapist: "So you feel like you failed" — the client didn't say "failed."

SDRS Manual v5.0 The concept of accurate reflection using client's exact words is from Padesky (2019), cited in the manual.

Clinical Knowledge The term "interpretive drift" and its detailed examples are clinical knowledge.

Stage 3: Summaries

Creates the visible foundation for discovery. Without a summary, the client must hold all information in memory while synthesizing — cognitively overwhelming.

SDRS Manual v5.0 The importance of summaries and their written form is directly from the manual (pp. 4-5).

Adapted The distinction between minor/major summaries in text format is an adaptation.

Stage 4: Analytical/Synthesizing Questions

Where discovery happens. Synthesizing = "How do these fit together?" Analytical = "How could you use this?"

SDRS Manual v5.0 The definitions and examples of analytical vs. synthesizing questions come directly from the manual (pp. 5-6).

▶ Expert-Level Markers Kazantzis et al. 2018 Clinical Knowledge
  1. Genuine Curiosity — The single best predictor (Padesky, 2020). Questions specific to THIS client, follows unexpected threads. SDRS Manual v5.0
  2. Strategic Question Sequencing — Concrete details → emotional experience → meanings → patterns → synthesis. Clinical Knowledge
  3. Seamless Stage Integration — Empathic listening woven throughout, minor summaries during gathering, analytical questions as natural culmination. Clinical Knowledge
  4. Client-Centered Discovery — Resists supplying insights. Discoveries attributed to the client. Kazantzis et al. 2018 Delphi panel identified the spectrum from therapist-suggested to client-discovered insights.
  5. Adaptive Flexibility — When client resists, rephrases rather than abandoning. Resistance met with curiosity. Clinical Knowledge

▶ PTSD-Specific Considerations Clinical Knowledge CPT literature
  • Evidence-based questioning: CPT stuck points, hindsight bias ("Seeing it with what you know NOW vs. THEN?"), responsibility attribution ("Who controlled whether those 15 meters were crossable?") Clinical Knowledge
  • Respecting the belief's function: Self-blame preserves sense of control. Good SD explores respectfully, doesn't attack. Clinical Knowledge
  • Pacing: SD with PTSD cannot be rushed. Space for emotional processing. CPT literature
  • Not invalidating: "Help me understand what being responsible means to you" NOT "But you know it wasn't your fault, right?" Clinical Knowledge
  • AI client factor: Yoav may respond more cooperatively than a real PTSD client. Rate technique independently. Adapted
▶ Genuine vs. Fake Discovery & "Yes, But..." Signal SDRS Manual v5.0 Kazantzis et al. 2018

Genuine Discovery

Client expresses surprise, uses own language, makes connections therapist didn't suggest, applies insight beyond immediate focus.

NOT Discovery

  • Compliance: Therapist states conclusion, client agrees
  • Rhetorical persuasion: Leading question leaves only one acceptable answer
  • Parroting: Client repeats therapist's phrasing without genuine engagement

Kazantzis et al. 2018 Delphi panel identified 3 types: therapist-suggested, therapist-guided, independently discovered.

The "Yes, But..." Signal

SDRS Manual v5.0 "When clients say 'Yes, but...' during SD this is most often a signal that the therapist has given advice or drawn conclusions for the client." Clients rarely say this when making their own discoveries.

▶ Research Evidence Multiple Studies
  • Braun & Strunk, 2015 Greater Socratic questioning predicts greater symptom reduction in CBT, independently of therapeutic alliance. 1 SD increase predicts 1.51-point BDI-II decrease next session.
  • Braun et al., 2022 The effect is mediated by cognitive change — SD works because it promotes client's own cognitive restructuring.
  • Flemotomos et al., 2022 Lyssn.io: AI assessment achieves 100% of human reliability on CTRS total scores. Validates AI-based therapy competence rating.
  • Kazantzis et al., 2018 Delphi panel on SD: identified stylistic aspects and the discovery spectrum.
  • Overholser, 2011 Collaborative empiricism, guided discovery, and the Socratic method as core CBT processes.

Definition SDRS Manual v5.0

Given the session context, informational questions asked were appropriate, asked with genuine curiosity, engaged the client, and pursued until sufficient detail was obtained.

Behavioral Anchors Adapted

Score 0 — Needs Significant Improvement
No informational questions, OR entirely closed-ended/leading/rhetorical/irrelevant. More advice-giving than information-gathering. Examples: "You should try to forgive yourself" (advice). "Don't you think it's time to move on?" (leading). Hebrew patterns: "?...אתה לא חושב ש" (leading), "...אתה צריך" (advice).
Score 1 — Needs Improvement
Some questions present but mostly closed-ended, minimal follow-up, checklist-like, surface-level. Examples: "Were you scared?" (closed). "How are you sleeping? And your appetite?" (checklist).
Score 2 — Competent
Several open-ended questions, some curiosity, some follow-up but inconsistent. Shows awareness of clinical framework. Topic from one angle. Examples: "What was going through your mind when that happened?" (good). Misses some relevant areas.
Score 3 — Strengths in Evidence
Predominantly open-ended with genuine curiosity. Good follow-up. Model-informed (CPT stuck points). Topic from 2+ angles. Minor gaps. Examples: "Tell me about the conditions when you tried to reach Nadav" (specific). "If another commander described the same situation, what would you think?" (multiple perspectives).
Score 4 — Outstanding Strength
All open-ended, genuinely curious. Excellent follow-up. Model + case conceptualization. 3+ angles. Notices what's missing. Adapts to resistance. Examples: "What does 'fast enough' look like in your mind?" (examining assumptions). "I notice you haven't mentioned what Nadav meant to you personally" (noticing absence).

Focus Dimensions (6) Adapted

A. Question Type (open/closed/leading/rhetorical) • B. Curiosity (genuine vs. procedural) • C. Follow-Up Depth • D. Clinical Relevance (model-informed) • E. Multiple Perspectives • F. Client Engagement (supplementary only)

File: prompts/rater_prompt_item2.md

Definition SDRS Manual v5.0

Therapist showed signs of appropriate empathy through language. Used accurate reflections incorporating client language (imagery, metaphors) without interpretive drift.

Text-Based Adaptation Adapted

Original SDRS includes nonverbal cues (facial expressions, vocal inflections, eye contact). In text, empathy assessed ONLY through: word choice, reflection accuracy, client language adoption, metaphor handling, noticing missing elements, timing/pacing.

Standard is HIGH because text carries the entire empathic signal. Generic "I understand" is weak evidence.

Behavioral Anchors Adapted

Score 0 — Needs Significant Improvement
No reflections, misnames emotions, ignores emotional content, uses only own language, robotic/detached. Example: Yoav shares guilt → therapist asks next question without acknowledgment.
Score 1 — Needs Improvement
Reflections present but inaccurate (interpretive drift). Wrong moments/intensity. Mostly therapist's words. Example: Yoav says "I should have reached him faster" → "So you feel like you failed as a commander" (added "failed" and "commander").
Score 2 — Competent
Several accurate reflections, some client language, generally appropriate empathy. Minor interpretive drift. No missing elements noticed. Reflections serve dialogue but don't deepen it.
Score 3 — Strengths in Evidence
Predominantly Yoav's exact language. Empathy matches mood. Metaphors adopted. Missing elements noticed. Accuracy checked once. Example: "You keep coming back to those 15 meters — they seem to carry a lot of weight."
Score 4 — Outstanding Strength
Consistently exact words. Precisely calibrated empathy. All metaphors adopted and explored. Every emotional moment acknowledged. Missing elements skillfully inquired about. Accuracy checked regularly. Example: "A wall — 15 meters felt like something impossible to get through. Tell me about that wall."

4
Ignoring missed emotional moments
5
Confusing empathy (Item 2) with summaries (Item 3)

File: prompts/rater_prompt_item3.md

Definition SDRS Manual v5.0

Therapist initiates summaries at appropriate times. Summaries accurately highlight the most important client observations in the client's own words and, when possible, are collaboratively created.

Text-Based Adaptation Adapted

In text, ALL therapist output is "written." A summary must be a DISTINCT, STRUCTURED CONSOLIDATION of multiple points from across the conversation — not just any sentence.

Critical test: Does this message synthesize information from MULTIPLE earlier points? If it only reflects one point, it's Item 2, not Item 3.

Minor summary: Briefly consolidates 2-3 points. Major summary: Comprehensive structured review of key themes.

Behavioral Anchors Adapted

Score 0 — Needs Significant Improvement
No summaries at all. Each turn independent. Conversation is entirely Q&A with no pauses to consolidate.
Score 1 — Needs Improvement
One brief summary but inaccurate/incomplete, misses key points, uses therapist's language, poor timing, no collaboration.
Score 2 — Competent
At least one structured summary. Captures main themes. Mostly Yoav's language. Reasonably accurate. Some collaboration attempt.
Score 3 — Strengths in Evidence
Well-structured comprehensive summary + minor summaries throughout. Accurate, Yoav's words. Collaborative. Includes emotions, not just facts. Sets up analytical questions.
Score 4 — Outstanding Strength
Multiple strategic summaries. Major summary is comprehensive, exact words, emotional nuance. Collaboratively constructed. Captures patterns/contradictions. Invites discovery.

2

3
Crediting client's self-summary as therapist behavior
4
Confusing long turn with summary
5
Over-crediting poorly timed summary (too early)

File: prompts/rater_prompt_item4.md

Definition SDRS Manual v5.0

After a period of SD, Analytical or Synthesizing questions are asked that require the client to consolidate learning and/or apply what was discussed. Questions link to evidence-based models, case conceptualization, and/or treatment plan.

Critical: Distinguishing from Item 1 Adapted Clinical Knowledge

The most common rating error. Decision rule: Does the question ask the client to REPORT (Item 1) or to DISCOVER (Item 4)?

DimensionItem 1: InformationalItem 4: Analytical/Synth.
FunctionGATHER dataPROCESS/CONNECT/APPLY
DirectionInfo flows FROM clientInsight flows WITHIN client
TimingEarlier in SDAfter info gathered
Client's taskReport, describe, recallAnalyze, synthesize, apply
Answers are...Already known to clientDiscovered in the moment

Leading Question Trap: "Don't you think the conditions made it impossible?" is NOT analytical — it's leading. True analytical questions are OPEN: "What do you notice when you compare the conditions to your expectation?"

Behavioral Anchors Adapted

Score 0 — Needs Significant Improvement
No analytical/synthesizing questions. Therapist draws conclusions, gives advice, or only asks informational questions.
Score 1 — Needs Improvement
One vague/generic analytical question. Not linked to specific content. Therapist partially answers own question. Poor timing. Takes credit for insights.
Score 2 — Competent
At least one clearly analytical question connected to discussion. Relevant to clinical model. Yoav given space. Limited follow-up. No reference to summary.
Score 3 — Strengths in Evidence
Multiple analytical/synthesizing questions building on discussion. Model-linked. References summary/key observations. Credits Yoav. Follow-up deepens answers.
Score 4 — Outstanding Strength
Multiple well-crafted synthesizing AND analytical questions. Precisely model-linked. References summary. Full space. Credits all discoveries. Adapts to resistance with alternative questions. Promotes hope and application.

Aggregation Pass

File: prompts/rater_prompt_aggregation.md — Pass 5, receives the 4 item rating JSONs.

What It Does Design Choice

  1. Compile scores — Extract Item 1-4 scores, calculate total (0-16)
  2. Consistency check — Flag genuine contradictions across items (diverse scores are normal)
  3. Competence threshold — Total ≥ 8 = MEETS; < 8 = DOES NOT MEET
  4. Developmental feedback — Strengths, development areas, 3 recommendations
  5. Session characterization — Which SD stages present/absent/partial

Rule: The aggregation pass does NOT re-rate. It uses scores as given. Design Choice

Output Format

{ "transcript_id": "T001", "scores": { "item1": 3, "item2": 2, "item3": 1, "item4": 2, "total": 8 }, "competence_threshold": { "meets_threshold": true, "threshold": 8 }, "developmental_feedback": { "primary_strengths": ["..."], "primary_development_areas": ["..."], "top_recommendations": ["..."] }, "summary_narrative": "..." }

Full 10-turn conversation in Hebrew

The API receives ~5,000 words of instructions + the transcript. Temperature is set to 0.0 for deterministic output.

2 Evidence Extraction (Step 2 of Rating Procedure)

Following the 6-step procedure from the system prompt, the rater first reads the full transcript (Step 1), then goes through EVERY therapist turn extracting quotes relevant to Item 1:

{ "turn": 3, "quote": "ספר לי יותר על הרגע שניסית להגיע לנדב. מה היו התנאים בשטח? מה ראית, מה שמעת?", "classification": "Open-ended, multi-angle informational question with sensory detail pursuit", "level": "Score 3: Specific, model-informed, multiple perspectives" }

This is repeated for ALL therapist turns, building a complete evidence table. The behavioral anchors from the item prompt guide the classification.

3 Identify Absent Behaviors

The rater checks: what SHOULD have been present for scores 3-4 but wasn't?

  • Present: Open-ended questions, sensory detail pursuit, multiple angles
  • Absent: No questions examining assumptions ("What does 'fast enough' mean to you?"), no noticing of what client didn't mention, no adaptation when client showed resistance

These absences are important evidence — they cap the score.

4 Determine Predominant Pattern

Looking across all 10 turns:

  • Turns 1-3: Good open-ended questions with follow-up
  • Turns 4-6: Some closed-ended questions creep in
  • Turns 7-10: Returns to open-ended but becomes more leading

Predominant pattern: "Competent with moments of strength. Mostly open-ended, shows genuine curiosity and some clinical awareness, but inconsistent follow-up and occasional drift toward leading questions."

5 Assign Score

Based on the evidence:

2
Competent

The evidence consistently points to Score 2: several open-ended questions, some curiosity, some follow-up but inconsistent.

6 Justify: Why Not Higher/Lower?

Why not Score 3? Would need: predominantly model-informed questions, topic explored from 2+ angles consistently, minimal gaps. The leading questions in turns 7-10 and inconsistent follow-up prevent a 3.

Why not Score 1? The open-ended questions in turns 1-3 show genuine curiosity beyond checklist questioning. Multiple perspectives attempted even if not sustained. Too much quality for a 1.
